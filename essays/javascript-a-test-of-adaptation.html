JavaScript: A Test of Adaptation

17 Jan 2023

Before I was an ICS major, I was a music composition major. I began studying music about 10 years ago, and studied it in college for 2.5 years. That’s when I decided that maybe I should switch my area of study.

Don’t get me wrong, I greatly enjoy music to this day, and it’s one of my favorite things in the world, but I felt that going into that world would consume me and I would not want to grow jaded to music ever.

I loved mathematics in high school, and I took many engineering courses as well. My friend suggested I study computer science, and I thought “why not.” I never took AP Computer Science in high school, but I was willing to learn.

Unsurprisingly, the switch from music to ICS was an abrupt one. Orchestration classes to discrete math classes. Brass methods became intro to computer science. Since I passed my AP Calculus test in high school, I was dropped right into calculus 2 with no review for 2.5 years.

Now, being in the ICS program for one year, I think that despite the drastic shift in courses, I do not regret changing my major. It has taught me to adapt and to catch up despite being 2.5 years behind.

But I think my ability to adapt is now being put to the test in this Software Engineering course.

Into the World of Javascript

Since this is only my second year into ICS, I only have a year of experience in the only coding language I know: Java. My experience only goes as far as the intro courses, and a few LeetCode problems along the way, so learning the basics of JavaScript within 142 short lessons in one weekend was fairly daunting.

However, it wasn’t so bad, but I don’t think I truly understand it yet. So far, I’ve been using JavaScript very similarly to how I use Java. Now that I know that functions are first-class, I don’t think I’m fully ready to use it. Maybe I need to review the 142 lessons again.

So far, without getting too deep into JavaScript just yet, I can’t say for sure whether it’s good or bad for software engineering, but I’m sure I can form a better opinion as I hone my JavaScript skills (I hope).

Practice, and lots of it.

If studying music in college for 2.5 years has taught me anything, it’s that I may be able to play one piece for an upcoming recital, but I need to play more pieces to actually get better at my instrument, rather than only getting better at the piece. Having good practice habits, learning to sight read, learning to improvise and many more skills build better musicianship alongside building a better player.

Athletic software engineering is a concept I never considered, but I now find it to be incredibly important. I could understand concepts of coding and algorithms, but these WODs and other short coding challenges are almost testing my “muscle memory” of coding.

The ability to code fast and on my toes puts everything I’ve learned, and everything I’ve practiced to the test. It doesn’t matter how much you “know,” but rather, how much time and effort you put into coding.

There have been instances when I have improvised solos for various music groups, and I just freeze up, or I play the same things over and over again, even though I studied lots of different techniques. It didn’t matter how many concepts or how much theory I knew, I didn’t practice them enough, and it showed. These WODs are a prime example of coding in action, and I’m fully on board.

Overall…

This journey into ICS and coding has felt like a test of my ability to adapt, and I feel like this class is like the exam.

From learning JavaScript in one weekend to the WODs, I feel like this class will grant me such valuable lessons to prepare me for whatever job I go into. I hope to be able to fully utilize JavaScript to it’s fullest potential, and to add it to my toolbox. I hope to do well in all of the WODs in this class and I plan to practice as much as I can.

I’m excited for this class. I’m sure it may be stressful at times, but it wouldn’t be rewarding if it wasn’t.
