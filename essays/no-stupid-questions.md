As a famous individual once said:

> "Remember, there are no stupid questions, only stupid people"
>                                 - Mr. Garrison (South Park)

In most cases, there are no stupid questions. It's fundamental to ask questions, and it's important to always question why things are the way they are.

However, it is very easy to take advantage of that idea. It's very easy to not think for yourself, and let others answer questions that have probably been answered many times before. Is it stupid? I guess not, but it could possibly make you seem that way. Asking smart questions is not just asking questions in a smart way. It engages the person asking the question to really think for themselves before taking someone else's time to answer it.

## Separating the Smart and Stupid

When looking into open source communities like Stack Overflow, there are an abundance of questions and discussions about numerous topics regarding programming. A community of users who are passionate about coding, and wish to share their knowledge with others, while also learning for themselves. Those who partake in this community and ask questions usually ask because they cannot find an answer (or an answer they understand) in other resources.

Take this user who asked about a way to [check if an array includes a value](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/237104/how-do-i-check-if-an-array-includes-a-value-in-javascript). First, they ask for a more efficient way to check an array, but then they include code, displaying the extent to which they understand the topic. They post the only wat they know how to do it, allowing discussion to build off of what they know, rather than off of nothing. They also include a reference to a similar question that looks into a similar topic.

Now, this isn't a perfect smart question. There are some cases that aren't specified or edited into the question that are brought up in the comments, but this engaged in intellectual discussion that brought a plethora of meaningful answers.

On a similar topic, this user asked [how to get and check array of array in JavaScript](https://stackoverflow.com/questions/25257308/get-and-check-javascript-array-of-array). Their title has poor grammar, they don't try to engage in conversation, they only ask the question. Other users were pretty quick to ask if they even tried to answer the question themselves before asking it. One user said "this looks like homework."

Inherently, there are no "stupid" questions, but it really does seem like this user wished to get a homework question correct, and used other people online for their knowledge, without ever returning anything, not even a "thank you." Now maybe this is an exaggeration, but it's one potential explanation for a user who really didn't put a lot of thought into asking a question, and I believe that is the root of a smart question.

A "smart" question, in essence, isn't just a carefully worded question as if one was e-mailing their boss. Rather, a "smart" question is a question that truly seeks understanding about a topic. It's a question that came from a perspective of someone seeking knowledge that they did search prior to asking. It shows respect for a community of knowledgable individuals regarding any topic, with intention to pay it back whenever they can. Maybe I'm hyperbolizing what a smart question is, but when one is truly passionate about something, these questions resemble a lot more than one may think.
